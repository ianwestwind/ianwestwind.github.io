Week 1 — Textbook

Bayes' Theorem:
Updating Beliefs with Evidence

What does it mean to learn from data? Bayesian inference offers a mathematically precise answer — and it starts with a deceptively simple formula.

1. The Problem of Uncertain Beliefs

Imagine you find a coin on the street. Is it a fair coin? You don't know. Maybe it's perfectly balanced, maybe it's loaded to land heads 70% of the time. Your prior belief is that it's probably fair — but you're not certain.

Now you flip it 10 times and get 8 heads. Should you update your belief? Of course. But by how much? This is precisely the question Bayesian inference answers.

2. The Formula

Bayes' Theorem tells us how to update a probability when we receive new evidence:

P(H | E) = P(E | H) × P(H) / P(E)
SymbolNameMeaning
P(H)PriorYour belief in H before seeing evidence
P(E|H)LikelihoodHow probable is the evidence if H is true?
P(H|E)PosteriorYour updated belief after seeing evidence E
P(E)MarginalTotal probability of the evidence (normalizing constant)

3. An Intuitive Example: The Coin

Let's define two hypotheses:

  • H₁: The coin is fair (P(heads) = 0.5)
  • H₂: The coin is biased (P(heads) = 0.7)

Suppose we start believing both hypotheses equally: P(H₁) = P(H₂) = 0.5. We flip the coin and get Heads.

Calculation
P(Heads | fair) = 0.5    P(Heads | biased) = 0.7

P(E) = 0.5 × 0.5 + 0.5 × 0.7 = 0.60

P(fair | Heads) = (0.5 × 0.5) / 0.60 ≈ 0.417
P(biased | Heads) = (0.7 × 0.5) / 0.60 ≈ 0.583

One heads made us slightly more convinced the coin is biased. Makes sense! Each flip is weak evidence, but evidence accumulates.

4. The Key Insight: Updating Is Iterative

The posterior from one update becomes the prior for the next update. This is the beating heart of Bayesian inference: beliefs evolve continuously as evidence arrives, making it perfectly suited to medical data streams, sensor readings, and longitudinal studies.

Why This Matters in Healthcare
A patient's test result doesn't exist in a vacuum. Bayesian reasoning lets us combine a test's sensitivity and specificity with the population prevalence of a disease to compute the true probability a positive test means the patient is sick — often strikingly different from what intuition suggests.

5. Frequentist vs. Bayesian Thinking

ApproachQuestion AskedWhat's Random?
Frequentist"What would happen over many repetitions?"The data
Bayesian"Given this data, what should I believe?"The hypothesis

Neither is universally superior — but Bayesian methods shine when prior knowledge is valuable and when you need to update beliefs sequentially.

6. Summary

Bayes' Theorem is a machine for rational belief revision. You start with a prior, multiply by the likelihood of observed evidence, and normalize. The result — the posterior — becomes your new, better-informed belief. Repeat with every new observation.

Question 1 — Model Answer
In your own words, explain prior, likelihood, and posterior.
Answer
The prior is your belief about the coin's fairness before any flips — e.g., "I think it's probably fair." The likelihood describes how probable the observed data (e.g., 8 heads in 10 flips) would be under a specific hypothesis (e.g., given that P(heads)=0.7). The posterior is your updated belief after combining the prior with the likelihood via Bayes' Theorem — it reflects what you now believe given the evidence. In the coin lab, the posterior distribution shifts toward higher P(heads) values when many heads are observed, reflecting rational belief revision.

Grading note: Award full credit for correct directional understanding. Accept varied wording. Deduct 3 pts if student confuses prior and posterior.
Question 2 — Model Answer
Disease base rate 1%, sensitivity 95%, specificity 90%.
Full Calculation
Let D = disease, T+ = positive test.

P(D) = 0.01    P(T+|D) = 0.95    P(T+|¬D) = 0.10

P(T+) = P(T+|D)·P(D) + P(T+|¬D)·P(¬D)
       = 0.95 × 0.01 + 0.10 × 0.99 = 0.0095 + 0.099 = 0.1085

P(D|T+) = (0.95 × 0.01) / 0.1085 ≈ 0.0876 ≈ 8.76%

Despite a 95% sensitive test, only ~9% of positives actually have the disease — because the disease is rare. This is the base rate fallacy.
Question 3 — Model Answer
Lab observation with bias=0.70, flat prior.
Expected Observations
After 50 flips: posterior mean should be approximately 0.63–0.72 (random variation).
After 100 flips: posterior mean should be closer to 0.70, with a narrower distribution.

Pattern: The posterior mean converges toward the true bias (0.70) as more data accumulates. The distribution also becomes narrower (more certain). This demonstrates the law of large numbers in a Bayesian framework.

Accept any reasonable empirical observation. Award full credit if student correctly describes convergence and reduced uncertainty.
Question 4 — Model Answer
Strong priors and evidence requirements in medicine.
Answer
A strong prior (e.g., concentrated Beta(20,20) belief in fairness) mathematically requires many data points to shift the posterior significantly, because the evidence must "outvote" the prior weight. In medicine, if a disease has prevalence of 0.01% (e.g., a rare genetic disorder), even a highly accurate test will produce mostly false positives in population screening — because P(Disease) in the prior is extremely low. The evidence of a positive test must contend with the strong prior probability of the patient not having the disease. This is why rare disease screening requires confirmation testing.
Week 2 — Textbook

The Beta Distribution:
A Prior Made for Probabilities

The Beta distribution is the natural choice for representing uncertainty about a probability. It lives between 0 and 1, and it updates beautifully when new data arrives.

1. Why We Need a Distribution for P

In Week 1, we treated our hypotheses as discrete ("fair" vs. "biased"). But in reality, the coin's bias θ is a continuous value between 0 and 1. We need a probability distribution that lives on [0,1] and can represent all our uncertainty about θ.

Enter the Beta distribution: Beta(α, β).

Beta(θ | α, β) ∝ θ^(α−1) · (1−θ)^(β−1)

2. Understanding α and β

Think of α and β as "pseudo-counts":

ParameterIntuitionEffect on Distribution
αPrior heads observationsShifts distribution right (toward 1)
βPrior tails observationsShifts distribution left (toward 0)
α+βTotal prior "confidence"Concentration: higher = narrower = more certain

A flat prior (total ignorance) is Beta(1,1) — a uniform distribution. "I believe the coin is fair, with moderate confidence" might be Beta(10,10).

3. The Magic: Conjugate Updating

The Beta distribution is a conjugate prior for the Binomial likelihood. This means: if you start with Beta(α, β) and observe h heads and t tails, your posterior is simply:

Posterior = Beta(α + h, β + t)

No integration. No numerical approximation. Just add your counts to the prior parameters. This is the mathematical elegance that makes Beta-Binomial updating so powerful and intuitive.

4. Python Implementation

PYTHON
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import beta as beta_dist
import random

# === PARAMETERS ===
TRUE_BIAS = 0.65        # The "true" bias of the coin (unknown to us)
ALPHA_PRIOR = 2        # Initial prior: pseudo-heads count
BETA_PRIOR  = 2        # Initial prior: pseudo-tails count
N_FLIPS = 100           # Number of coin flips to simulate

def flip_coin(p_heads=0.5):
    """Simulate a single biased coin flip."""
    return 1 if random.random() < p_heads else 0

def update_posterior(alpha, beta_param, outcome):
    """Update Beta parameters given a coin flip outcome (1=H, 0=T)."""
    if outcome == 1:   # Heads
        alpha += 1
    else:              # Tails
        beta_param += 1
    return alpha, beta_param

# === SIMULATION ===
alpha, beta_param = ALPHA_PRIOR, BETA_PRIOR
theta = np.linspace(0, 1, 200)
snapshots = [(0, alpha, beta_param)]

for i in range(N_FLIPS):
    outcome = flip_coin(TRUE_BIAS)
    alpha, beta_param = update_posterior(alpha, beta_param, outcome)
    if (i + 1) in [5, 10, 25, 50, 100]:
        snapshots.append((i + 1, alpha, beta_param))

# === PLOTTING ===
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, len(snapshots), figsize=(18, 4))
for ax, (n, a, b) in zip(axes, snapshots):
    y = beta_dist.pdf(theta, a, b)
    ax.fill_between(theta, y, alpha=0.3, color='steelblue')
    ax.plot(theta, y, color='steelblue')
    ax.axvline(TRUE_BIAS, color='red', ls='--', alpha=0.7)
    ax.set_title(f'n={n}\nBeta({a},{b})')
    ax.set_xlabel('θ (P heads)')
plt.suptitle('Posterior Evolution: Beta-Binomial Updating', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('posterior_evolution.png', dpi=150)
plt.show()

5. Java Implementation

JAVA
import org.apache.commons.math3.distribution.BetaDistribution;
import java.util.Random;

public class BayesianCoinFlip {
    private double alpha;
    private double beta;
    private final double trueBias;
    private final Random rng = new Random();

    public BayesianCoinFlip(double alpha, double beta, double trueBias) {
        this.alpha    = alpha;
        this.beta     = beta;
        this.trueBias = trueBias;
    }

    /** Simulate one coin flip and update the posterior. */
    public boolean flipAndUpdate() {
        boolean heads = rng.nextDouble() < trueBias;
        if (heads) alpha++; else beta++;
        return heads;
    }

    public double posteriorMean() { return alpha / (alpha + beta); }
    public double posteriorVariance() {
        double n = alpha + beta;
        return (alpha * beta) / (n * n * (n + 1));
    }

    public static void main(String[] args) {
        BayesianCoinFlip sim = new BayesianCoinFlip(2, 2, 0.65);
        int[] snappoints = {5, 10, 25, 50, 100};
        int next = 0;
        for (int i = 1; i <= 100; i++) {
            sim.flipAndUpdate();
            if (next < snappoints.length && i == snappoints[next]) {
                System.out.printf("n=%3d | Mean: %.3f | Var: %.5f%n",
                    i, sim.posteriorMean(), sim.posteriorVariance());
                next++;
            }
        }
    }
}

import random

def flip_and_update(alpha, beta, true_bias):
    outcome = 1 if random.random() < true_bias else 0
    if outcome == 1:
        alpha += 1
    else:
        beta += 1
    return alpha, beta

Week 3 — Textbook

Bayesian Inference
in Precision Health

From coin flips to clinical decisions — Bayesian reasoning is transforming how we diagnose disease, personalize treatment, and interpret biomarker data.

1. The Clinical Relevance of Base Rates

In Week 1, we showed that even a 95%-accurate test for a 1% prevalence disease is only 8.76% likely to be correct on a positive result. This is not a flaw in the test — it's a fundamental property of probability that physicians must internalize.

The Base Rate Fallacy
Studies show that many physicians intuitively give test sensitivity and specificity more weight than disease prevalence when interpreting test results. Bayesian literacy directly counters this dangerous cognitive error.

2. Sensitivity, Specificity, and Predictive Values

MetricFormulaBayesian Interpretation
Sensitivity (TPR)TP / (TP + FN)P(Test+ | Disease+) = Likelihood
Specificity (TNR)TN / (TN + FP)1 − P(Test+ | Disease−)
PPVTP / (TP + FP)P(Disease+ | Test+) = Posterior
NPVTN / (TN + FN)P(Disease− | Test−) = Posterior

PPV and NPV are the clinically actionable numbers — and both depend critically on disease prevalence (the prior).

3. Sequential Updating in Patient Care

Bayesian updating mirrors how good clinicians think. A physician's initial assessment ("fever + cough → 30% probability of pneumonia") is a prior. A chest X-ray is evidence. The updated probability is the posterior. A positive culture is more evidence. The final treatment decision is informed by the final posterior.

4. Precision Medicine Applications

  • Pharmacogenomics — Bayesian models update drug response probabilities given genotype
  • Cancer screening — Combining PSA, family history, and imaging with Bayesian networks
  • Wearable sensor data — Continuously updating arrhythmia risk from ECG streams
  • Clinical trial design — Bayesian adaptive trials can stop early for efficacy or futility
  • Digital phenotyping — Updating behavioral models from smartphone sensor data over time

5. The Bayesian Brain

Remarkably, neuroscientists now believe the brain itself performs Bayesian inference — combining prior expectations (based on experience) with sensory evidence to construct perception. In this sense, Bayesian thinking is not just a statistical method — it's how intelligence works.

"Bayesian inference is the mathematics of learning. It describes, in a unified framework, how any rational agent — human, clinical algorithm, or AI — should update beliefs when new information arrives."

Question 1 — Case Study Answers
Part (a): First Positive Test
P(D) = 0.10, P(+|D) = 0.90, P(+|¬D) = 0.15
P(+) = 0.90×0.10 + 0.15×0.90 = 0.09 + 0.135 = 0.225
P(D|+) = 0.09 / 0.225 = 0.400 (40%)
Part (b): Second Positive Test — Use posterior from (a) as new prior
New prior = 0.40
P(+) = 0.90×0.40 + 0.15×0.60 = 0.36 + 0.09 = 0.45
P(D|++) = 0.36 / 0.45 = 0.800 (80%)
Part (c): Explanation
Each positive test updates the prior upward. The second test starts from a much higher prior (40% vs 10%), so the same likelihood ratio produces a much larger jump. The multiplicative nature of Bayesian updating means early tests are relatively weak (fighting against a strong "no disease" prior), while later tests operate from a higher baseline and can dramatically concentrate probability. This is why confirmatory testing is so powerful — each result compounds the certainty from the last.
Question 2 — Reference Code
Python Solution
import random
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def run_diagnostic_simulation(prevalence, sensitivity, specificity, has_disease, n_tests=5):
    fp_rate = 1 - specificity
    prior = prevalence
    history = [prior]

    for _ in range(n_tests):
        # Simulate test outcome
        if has_disease:
            test_pos = random.random() < sensitivity
        else:
            test_pos = random.random() < fp_rate

        if test_pos:
            likelihood = sensitivity
            likelihood_neg = fp_rate
        else:
            likelihood = 1 - sensitivity
            likelihood_neg = specificity

        # Bayes update
        numerator = likelihood * prior
        denominator = numerator + likelihood_neg * (1 - prior)
        posterior = numerator / denominator
        history.append(posterior)
        prior = posterior

    return history

history = run_diagnostic_simulation(0.10, 0.90, 0.85, has_disease=True)
plt.plot(range(len(history)), [h*100 for h in history], 'o-', color='purple')
plt.xlabel('Tests Completed')
plt.ylabel('P(Disease) %')
plt.title('Sequential Bayesian Diagnostic Updating')
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.savefig('diagnostic_trajectory.png', dpi=150)
plt.show()

Model Answer
In digital phenotyping for depression monitoring, smartphone sensor data (movement, screen time, call frequency) serves as ongoing evidence. The prior might be established from a patient's clinical history — a known baseline depression risk score. The likelihood is derived from a trained model of how behavioral signals correlate with depressive episodes. The posterior updates daily, giving clinicians a continuously refined estimate of current mental health status. This could enable early intervention — alerting care teams when posterior risk crosses a threshold — before the patient presents in crisis, fundamentally shifting care from reactive to proactive.
